Big Ball of Mud Code Reads #12 Summary

by Ian McLuskie

This is a Summary of the Blog Post titled Code Reads #12: “Big Ball of Mud” by Scott Rosenberg.

        The “Big Ball of Mud” in the paper with the same name refers to how, often “the most frequently deployed of software architectures” tend to be “haphazardly structured, sprawling, sloppy, duct-tape and baling wire, spaghetti-code jungle[s].” Almost wherever you look within the programming field, this continues to be the case. The paper supposes that the reason why there are so many big balls of mud rolling around is because, more than anything they work. Not only that, they also get to market much faster and easier than their overly-planned but very well planned out counterparts. Over all, the distinct tendency towards these type of structureless-structures comes down to pure pragmatism that many bosses are forced to face.
        Even aside from the market benefits, often big balls of mud are much easier to modify, while in more planned structures modifying “forces acting between two parts of the system” can often undermine the whole larger architecture. Big balls of mud, because they are often pushed out sooner, also have the added benefit of much more user feedback to decide on features and improve existing ones. With this constant user feedback they are able to deliver more functional and useful, even if its not as unbuggy or clean. Over all, Rosenburg encourages us to remember that big balls of mud have their useful applications and advantages, and as a result should not be overlooked.
